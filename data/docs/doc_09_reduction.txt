TOPIC: Reduction

Reduction is the process of simmering or boiling a liquid to evaporate water content, concentrating flavors and thickening the consistency.

HOW IT WORKS:
Heat causes water molecules to evaporate. As water leaves, the remaining liquid becomes more concentrated — flavors intensify, sugars concentrate, and the liquid thickens naturally without adding a thickening agent.

COMMON REDUCTIONS:
- Stock reduction → glace (stock reduced by 90%, syrupy and intensely flavored)
- Wine reduction → base for pan sauces
- Cream reduction → thickened cream sauce
- Balsamic reduction → syrupy condiment
- Tomato sauce reduction → concentrated tomato flavor

HOW TO REDUCE:
1. Choose a wide, shallow pan — more surface area = faster evaporation
2. Simmer, not boil — boiling can make sauces cloudy and bitter
3. Skim foam and fat that rise to the surface
4. Stir occasionally to prevent scorching
5. Check consistency by coating the back of a spoon

REDUCTION RATIOS:
- Reduce by half: simmer until volume is 50% of starting amount
- Reduce by two-thirds: simmer until volume is 33% of starting amount
- Glace: reduce stock by 90%

TIPS:
- Season AFTER reducing — salt concentrates along with everything else
- Start with good quality liquid — reduction amplifies both good and bad flavors
- Use a ruler or finger to mark starting level in the pot
- Reduction continues slightly off heat — remove before target consistency

PAN SAUCE METHOD:
1. Sear protein, remove from pan
2. Pour off excess fat, leaving fond (browned bits)
3. Deglaze with wine or stock
4. Reduce by half, scraping up fond
5. Add cream or butter, swirl to finish
