TOPIC: Tempering Chocolate

Tempering is the process of heating and cooling chocolate to specific temperatures to stabilize the cocoa butter crystals. Properly tempered chocolate has a glossy finish, a satisfying snap, and melts smoothly in the mouth.

WHY TEMPERING MATTERS:
Cocoa butter can form 6 different crystal structures. Only Form V (beta crystals) produces the glossy, snappy chocolate we want. If chocolate is melted and cooled without tempering, it forms unstable crystals — resulting in dull, streaky, soft chocolate.

SIGNS OF UNTEMPERED CHOCOLATE:
- Dull, matte finish
- White streaks or bloom (fat migration to surface)
- Soft, crumbly texture
- Does not snap cleanly

TEMPERING TEMPERATURES (dark chocolate):
- Melt to: 115-120°F (46-49°C) — destroys all existing crystals
- Cool to: 82°F (28°C) — stable Form V crystals begin to form
- Rewarm to: 88-90°F (31-32°C) — working temperature

MILK AND WHITE CHOCOLATE:
- Lower temperatures throughout (more sensitive to heat)
- Milk chocolate working temp: 84-86°F
- White chocolate working temp: 82-84°F

TABLING METHOD:
1. Melt chocolate to 115°F
2. Pour 2/3 onto a marble slab
3. Spread and work with a spatula until cooled to 82°F
4. Return to remaining 1/3 warm chocolate
5. Stir to reach 88-90°F working temperature

SEEDING METHOD (easier):
1. Melt 2/3 of chocolate to 115°F
2. Remove from heat
3. Add finely chopped tempered chocolate (seeds) — 1/3 of total
4. Stir constantly until all melted and temperature reaches 88-90°F

TEST: Dip a knife tip — tempered chocolate sets in 3-5 minutes with a glossy finish.
