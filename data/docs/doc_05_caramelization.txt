TOPIC: Caramelization

Caramelization is the browning of sugar through heat. It is a purely chemical process — no proteins required — that produces hundreds of new flavor compounds with complex, nutty, and slightly bitter notes.

HOW IT WORKS:
When sugar is heated above its melting point, it undergoes pyrolysis — molecules break apart and recombine into new compounds. Different sugars caramelize at different temperatures:
- Fructose: 230°F (110°C)
- Glucose: 320°F (160°C)
- Sucrose (table sugar): 340°F (170°C)
- Lactose (milk sugar): 390°F (200°C)

CARAMELIZATION VS MAILLARD REACTION:
- Caramelization: sugar only, no protein needed
- Maillard reaction: requires both sugar and protein (amino acids)
- Both produce browning but different flavors

HOW TO CARAMELIZE SUGAR (wet method):
1. Combine sugar and a small amount of water in a heavy saucepan
2. Heat over medium-high without stirring until sugar dissolves
3. Once boiling, do not stir — swirl the pan gently instead
4. Watch color carefully: pale gold → amber → deep amber → burnt
5. Remove from heat just before desired color — residual heat continues cooking
6. For caramel sauce: add warm cream carefully (it will bubble violently)

HOW TO CARAMELIZE ONIONS:
1. Slice onions thinly and evenly
2. Cook in butter or oil over medium-low heat
3. Stir occasionally — not constantly
4. Cook 45-60 minutes until deep golden brown and jammy
5. Add a pinch of salt to draw out moisture
6. Do not rush with high heat — onions will burn not caramelize

COMMON MISTAKES:
- Stirring wet caramel — causes crystallization
- Too high heat — outside burns before inside caramelizes
- Calling browned onions done at 10 minutes — true caramelization takes 45+ minutes
