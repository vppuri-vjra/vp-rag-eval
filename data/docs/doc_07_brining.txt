TOPIC: Brining

Brining is the process of soaking meat in a saltwater solution (wet brine) or coating it with salt (dry brine) before cooking. It improves moisture retention and seasons the meat throughout — not just on the surface.

HOW WET BRINING WORKS:
Salt draws moisture out of meat through osmosis initially, then the brine is drawn back in carrying salt with it. The salt denatures proteins, causing them to retain more water during cooking. Result: juicier meat.

WET BRINE RATIO:
- Basic brine: 1 cup kosher salt per 1 gallon of water
- Sugar optional: ½ cup per gallon (promotes browning, adds subtle sweetness)
- Add aromatics: peppercorns, bay leaves, garlic, herbs

WET BRINE TIMING:
- Chicken pieces: 1-4 hours
- Whole chicken: 8-24 hours
- Turkey: 12-48 hours
- Pork chops: 1-4 hours
- Shrimp: 15-30 minutes only — longer makes them mushy

HOW DRY BRINING WORKS:
Salt is applied directly to the surface. It draws out moisture initially, which dissolves the salt. This brine is then reabsorbed, seasoning the meat deeply. The surface dries out — producing better browning than wet brining.

DRY BRINE METHOD:
1. Apply kosher salt generously to all surfaces (about ¾ tsp per pound)
2. Place uncovered on a rack in the refrigerator
3. Rest at least 1 hour, ideally overnight or up to 3 days
4. Do not rinse before cooking — the surface should be dry

WET VS DRY BRINE:
- Wet brine: better moisture retention, less browning
- Dry brine: better crust and browning, slightly less moisture
- Dry brine preferred for poultry skin — wet brine makes skin rubbery

IMPORTANT:
- Do not brine kosher or pre-salted meat — it will be too salty
- Always brine in the refrigerator — never at room temperature
- Rinse after wet brining to remove excess surface salt
