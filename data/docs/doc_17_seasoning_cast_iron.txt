TOPIC: Seasoning Cast Iron

Seasoning a cast iron pan is the process of baking thin layers of oil onto the surface to create a natural, non-stick coating called polymerized oil or seasoning. This protects the pan from rust and creates a smooth cooking surface.

HOW SEASONING WORKS:
When oil is heated above its smoke point in a thin layer, the fatty acid chains polymerize — they link together forming a hard, plastic-like coating that bonds to the iron. Multiple layers build up over time to create a smooth, durable surface.

INITIAL SEASONING (new or stripped pan):
1. Wash pan with soap and hot water — only time soap is acceptable
2. Dry completely — heat on stove for 2 minutes to evaporate all moisture
3. Apply a very thin layer of oil (flaxseed, vegetable, or shortening) to entire surface including outside and handle
4. Wipe off almost all the oil — it should look nearly dry (thick layers gum up)
5. Place upside down in oven at 450-500°F
6. Bake for 1 hour
7. Turn off oven, let pan cool inside
8. Repeat 3-6 times for a good base layer

BEST OILS FOR SEASONING:
- Flaxseed oil: creates hard, durable seasoning (but can flake)
- Crisco/vegetable shortening: traditional, reliable
- Vegetable or canola oil: widely used, good results
- Avoid: olive oil (low smoke point), butter (burns)

ONGOING CARE:
- Clean with hot water and a stiff brush — avoid soap after seasoning
- Dry immediately and thoroughly — cast iron rusts fast
- Apply a thin oil wipe after each use while still warm
- Cook fatty foods (bacon, sausage) to build seasoning naturally

WHAT DAMAGES SEASONING:
- Soaking in water or dishwasher
- Cooking highly acidic foods (tomatoes, citrus) for long periods
- Metal scourers or steel wool
- Leaving wet or damp

RESTORING RUSTY CAST IRON:
1. Scrub with steel wool until rust is gone
2. Wash, dry completely
3. Re-season from scratch following initial seasoning steps
