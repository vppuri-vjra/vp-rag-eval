TOPIC: Making a Roux

A roux is a cooked mixture of equal parts fat and flour by weight, used as a thickening agent for sauces, soups, and gravies. It is the foundation of classical French mother sauces.

HOW IT WORKS:
Flour contains starch granules. When cooked in fat, the starch granules are coated in fat, separating them so they do not clump when liquid is added. When liquid is added to the roux and heated, the starch granules absorb the liquid and swell, thickening the sauce.

RATIO:
- Equal parts fat and flour by weight
- Example: 2 tbsp butter + 2 tbsp flour thickens 1 cup of liquid to medium consistency

TYPES OF ROUX BY COLOR:
1. White roux: cooked 2-3 minutes, pale color, maximum thickening power — used in béchamel
2. Blonde roux: cooked 5-7 minutes, golden color, slightly nutty — used in velouté
3. Brown roux: cooked 15-20 minutes, medium brown, rich flavor — used in espagnole
4. Dark roux: cooked 30-45 minutes, very dark, deep flavor, minimal thickening — used in Cajun gumbo

HOW TO MAKE A ROUX:
1. Melt fat (butter, oil, or lard) in heavy saucepan over medium heat
2. Add flour all at once
3. Stir constantly with a wooden spoon or whisk
4. Cook to desired color, stirring continuously — do not let it sit
5. Add liquid (warm or hot) gradually while whisking constantly
6. Bring to a simmer, whisking until smooth and thickened

ADDING LIQUID:
- Add warm or hot liquid — cold liquid causes lumps
- Add gradually in stages — not all at once
- Whisk constantly while adding
- Bring to a simmer to fully activate thickening

COMMON MISTAKES:
- Unequal parts fat and flour — grainy or greasy sauce
- Adding cold liquid — causes lumps that are hard to remove
- Not cooking long enough — raw flour taste remains
- Stopping stirring — burns on the bottom

FAT OPTIONS:
- Butter: classic, rich flavor (clarified butter for dark roux — regular butter burns)
- Vegetable oil: neutral, good for dark roux
- Lard or duck fat: traditional in regional cooking
