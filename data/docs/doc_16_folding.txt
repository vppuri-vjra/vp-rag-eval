TOPIC: Folding vs Stirring

Folding is a gentle mixing technique used to combine a light, airy ingredient (like whipped cream or beaten egg whites) with a heavier mixture without deflating the air bubbles that give volume and lightness.

WHY FOLDING MATTERS:
Beaten egg whites and whipped cream contain thousands of tiny air bubbles trapped in protein or fat networks. Stirring vigorously breaks these bubbles — destroying the volume and lightness you worked to create. Folding preserves them.

HOW TO FOLD PROPERLY:
1. Add lighter mixture on top of heavier mixture
2. Use a large rubber spatula
3. Cut down through the center of the bowl to the bottom
4. Sweep along the bottom and up the side
5. Rotate the bowl slightly
6. Repeat — each fold incorporates mixture gently
7. Stop as soon as just combined — streaks are acceptable
8. Do not overfold — deflates and toughens

WHEN TO FOLD VS STIR:
- FOLD: soufflés, mousse, chiffon cake, angel food cake, macarons, cheesecake with whipped cream
- STIR: anything where air incorporation is not critical — sauces, batters where gluten development is desired

FOLDING BEATEN EGG WHITES:
- Add 1/3 of whites to base mixture first — stir this in (lightens the base)
- Add remaining whites in two additions — fold gently
- A few white streaks remaining are fine — overmixing is worse

COMMON MISTAKES:
- Using a whisk instead of spatula — too aggressive
- Stirring in circles — breaks bubbles and develops gluten
- Adding all whites at once — too heavy to fold gently
- Overfolding until perfectly smooth — mixture deflates

FOLDING IN BAKING GENERALLY:
The same principle applies to any batter where minimal gluten development is desired (muffins, pancakes, quick breads). Overmixing develops gluten — creating tough, dense baked goods instead of tender ones. Mix until just combined — lumps are acceptable.
