TOPIC: Emulsification

Emulsification is the process of combining two liquids that do not naturally mix — typically oil and water — into a stable, uniform mixture called an emulsion.

HOW IT WORKS:
Oil and water repel each other. An emulsifier is a molecule with one end that attracts water and one end that attracts fat. It surrounds oil droplets and keeps them suspended in water (or vice versa), preventing separation.

COMMON EMULSIFIERS:
- Lecithin (found in egg yolks) — used in mayonnaise, hollandaise, aioli
- Mustard — contains mucilage that acts as an emulsifier, used in vinaigrettes
- Honey — used in salad dressings
- Garlic — contains natural emulsifying compounds

TYPES OF EMULSIONS:
- Temporary: vinaigrette shaken before use — separates quickly
- Semi-permanent: mustard vinaigrette — holds for hours
- Permanent: mayonnaise — stable indefinitely if refrigerated

HOW TO MAKE MAYONNAISE (classic emulsion):
1. Whisk egg yolks with mustard and a little vinegar
2. Add oil drop by drop while whisking constantly — this is critical
3. As emulsion forms, oil can be added in a thin stream
4. Season with salt and lemon juice

BROKEN EMULSIONS:
An emulsion breaks when oil droplets coalesce back together.
- Caused by: adding oil too fast, too much oil, temperature changes
- Fix: start fresh with a new yolk and slowly whisk the broken emulsion into it

COOKING APPLICATIONS:
- Mayonnaise and aioli
- Hollandaise and béarnaise sauce
- Vinaigrette
- Butter sauces (beurre blanc)
- Chocolate ganache
