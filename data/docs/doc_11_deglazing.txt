TOPIC: Deglazing

Deglazing is the process of adding liquid to a hot pan after searing or sautéing to dissolve the browned bits (fond) stuck to the bottom. These bits are concentrated flavor and form the base of pan sauces.

WHAT IS FOND:
Fond (French for "base" or "bottom") refers to the browned, caramelized bits of protein and sugars that stick to the pan during high-heat cooking. They are the product of the Maillard reaction and caramelization — packed with deep, savory flavor.

HOW TO DEGLAZE:
1. Remove food from pan — leave the fond behind
2. Pour off excess fat if needed (leave 1-2 tbsp)
3. With pan still hot over medium-high heat, add liquid
4. It will sizzle and steam vigorously
5. Use a wooden spoon or spatula to scrape up all the fond
6. Stir until fond is fully dissolved into the liquid
7. Reduce the liquid to concentrate flavor

BEST DEGLAZING LIQUIDS:
- Wine (red for beef, white for chicken/fish/pork) — adds acidity and complexity
- Stock (chicken, beef, vegetable) — adds body
- Beer — adds malty depth (good with pork or sausage)
- Juice (apple, orange) — adds sweetness and acidity
- Vinegar — highly acidic, use sparingly, good for quick pan sauces
- Water — works but adds no additional flavor

BUILDING A PAN SAUCE AFTER DEGLAZING:
1. Deglaze with wine — reduce by half
2. Add stock — reduce by half again
3. Optional: add cream for richness
4. Finish with cold butter (monter au beurre) — swirl in off heat for gloss and body
5. Season with salt and pepper
6. Strain through fine mesh sieve for elegant sauce

DO NOT:
- Add cold liquid to a scorching pan — risk of warping
- Skip scraping the fond — that is where all the flavor lives
- Over-reduce — sauce becomes too salty and intense
