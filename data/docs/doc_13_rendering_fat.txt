TOPIC: Rendering Fat

Rendering is the process of slowly cooking animal fat over low heat to melt it and separate the pure liquid fat from connective tissue, water, and proteins. The result is a clean, flavorful cooking fat.

COMMON RENDERED FATS:
- Lard: rendered pork fat — high smoke point (370°F), excellent for frying and pastry
- Duck fat: rendered duck or goose fat — prized for roasting potatoes, confit
- Beef tallow: rendered beef fat — very high smoke point (400°F+), used for frying
- Schmaltz: rendered chicken fat — used in Jewish cooking for matzo balls and sautéing
- Bacon fat: rendered as bacon cooks — flavor-packed cooking fat

HOW TO RENDER FAT:
1. Cut fat into small, uniform pieces (smaller = faster rendering)
2. Place in heavy pot or pan with a splash of water (prevents scorching at start)
3. Cook over low heat — patience is essential
4. Fat will melt slowly, releasing liquid fat
5. Connective tissue (cracklings) will brown and crisp up
6. Strain through fine mesh sieve or cheesecloth
7. Cool and store in sealed container in refrigerator (months) or freezer (years)

WET VS DRY RENDERING:
- Wet: start with a little water — gentler, lighter colored fat, less flavor
- Dry: no water — more flavorful, slightly darker fat, more attention needed

CRACKLINGS:
The solid bits left after rendering are cracklings (or pork rinds). Season with salt while hot — they are a delicious snack.

SMOKE POINTS OF RENDERED FATS:
- Chicken schmaltz: 375°F
- Lard: 370°F
- Duck fat: 375°F
- Beef tallow: 400°F+

WHY RENDERED FAT IS VALUED:
- High smoke points for frying
- Rich, savory flavor that vegetable oils lack
- Long shelf life when properly stored
- Traditional use in pastry (lard makes the flakiest pie crust)
