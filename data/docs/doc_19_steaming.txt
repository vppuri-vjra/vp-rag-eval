TOPIC: Steaming

Steaming is a moist-heat cooking method where food is cooked by exposure to steam from boiling water. Food does not touch the water — only the steam contacts it.

PURPOSE:
- Preserves nutrients better than boiling (water-soluble vitamins do not leach out)
- Retains natural color, flavor, and texture of vegetables
- Cooks delicate foods gently without agitation
- Fat-free cooking method

HOW STEAMING WORKS:
Water boils at 212°F and produces steam. Steam condenses on food, transferring heat. Because food is not submerged, nutrients and flavor compounds stay in the food rather than leaching into water.

STEAMING EQUIPMENT:
- Bamboo steamer: traditional, stacks multiple tiers, absorbs excess moisture
- Metal steamer basket: fits inside a pot, adjustable
- Steamer insert: pot with perforated insert
- Improvised: colander over a pot of boiling water, covered with a lid
- Electric steamer: countertop appliance

HOW TO STEAM:
1. Add 1-2 inches of water to pot — water should not touch the steamer basket
2. Bring water to a boil
3. Place food in steamer basket in a single layer if possible
4. Cover tightly — steam must not escape
5. Cook to desired doneness
6. Check water level for long cooking — refill with boiling water if needed

STEAMING TIMES:
- Broccoli florets: 5-6 minutes
- Green beans: 4-5 minutes
- Asparagus: 4-5 minutes
- Fish fillet (1 inch thick): 8-10 minutes
- Chicken breast: 15-20 minutes
- Dumplings: 8-10 minutes
- Rice (in bamboo steamer): 20-25 minutes after soaking

STEAMING VS BOILING:
- Steaming: food above water, nutrients stay in food, gentler
- Boiling: food submerged, some nutrients leach into water, more agitation

FLAVOR ENHANCEMENT:
- Add aromatics to the water: ginger, lemongrass, wine, herbs
- Steam will carry subtle flavor into the food
- Place aromatics directly under or around food in the basket
