TOPIC: Poaching

Poaching is a moist-heat cooking method where food is submerged in liquid kept at a gentle temperature of 160-180°F (71-82°C) — below simmering (185°F) and well below boiling (212°F).

PURPOSE:
- Cooks delicate proteins gently without toughening them
- Keeps food moist and tender
- Infuses subtle flavor from the poaching liquid

TEMPERATURE GUIDE:
- Poaching: 160-180°F — small bubbles occasionally break the surface
- Simmering: 185-200°F — steady stream of small bubbles
- Boiling: 212°F — vigorous rolling bubbles

BEST FOODS FOR POACHING:
- Eggs (classic poached egg)
- Fish and seafood (salmon, halibut, shrimp)
- Chicken breast (stays moist unlike boiling)
- Fruit (pears, peaches in spiced syrup)

POACHING LIQUIDS:
- Court bouillon: water + white wine + aromatics (for fish)
- Chicken stock (for chicken)
- Milk (for delicate fish)
- Red wine + spices (for pears)
- Plain salted water + vinegar (for eggs)

HOW TO POACH EGGS:
1. Fill pan with 3 inches of water, add 1 tbsp white vinegar
2. Heat to just below simmering — small bubbles on bottom
3. Crack egg into a small cup first
4. Create a gentle whirlpool with a spoon
5. Slide egg into center of whirlpool
6. Cook 3-4 minutes for set white, runny yolk
7. Remove with slotted spoon, drain on paper towel

WHY VINEGAR HELPS:
Acid speeds up protein coagulation — the white sets faster and stays together rather than spreading into wispy threads.

SHALLOW POACHING (fish):
Fish is partially submerged, covered with parchment, cooked in oven at 325°F. The steam from the liquid cooks the top. Poaching liquid becomes the sauce.
