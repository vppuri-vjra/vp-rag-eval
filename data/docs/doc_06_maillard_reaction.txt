TOPIC: Maillard Reaction

The Maillard reaction is a chemical reaction between amino acids (proteins) and reducing sugars that produces the brown color and complex flavors associated with cooked food. Named after French chemist Louis-Camille Maillard who described it in 1912.

HOW IT WORKS:
At temperatures above 280°F (140°C), amino acids and sugars react to form hundreds of new flavor and aroma compounds. The reaction produces melanoidins — brown-colored polymers responsible for the crust on bread, the sear on steak, and the color of roasted coffee.

MAILLARD VS CARAMELIZATION:
- Maillard: requires protein AND sugar — happens on meat, bread, roasted nuts
- Caramelization: sugar only — happens in candy making, onions
- Both produce browning but through different chemistry

CONDITIONS REQUIRED:
- Temperature: above 280°F (140°C) — cannot occur in boiling water (max 212°F)
- Low moisture: surface must be dry — wet surfaces steam instead of brown
- Time: surface must stay in contact with heat long enough

HOW TO MAXIMIZE MAILLARD REACTION:
1. Pat food completely dry before cooking — moisture is the enemy
2. Preheat pan until very hot before adding food
3. Do not overcrowd the pan — crowding creates steam
4. Use a fat with high smoke point
5. Do not move food too soon — let crust form before turning
6. Season with salt after searing (salt draws moisture)

FOODS WHERE MAILLARD IS CRITICAL:
- Steaks and burgers (crust)
- Bread crust and toast
- Roasted coffee and cocoa
- Fried foods
- Roasted vegetables

WHY BOILING OR STEAMING CANNOT BROWN FOOD:
Water boils at 212°F — far below the 280°F needed for Maillard. This is why boiled chicken looks grey and roasted chicken looks brown.
