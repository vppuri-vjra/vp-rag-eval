TOPIC: Braising

Braising is a combination cooking method that uses both dry and moist heat. Food is first seared at high heat, then cooked slowly in a covered pot with liquid at low heat.

PURPOSE:
- Tenderizes tough, collagen-rich cuts of meat (short ribs, brisket, lamb shank, pork shoulder)
- Develops deep, complex flavor through long slow cooking
- Collagen breaks down into gelatin, creating rich, silky braising liquid

HOW TO BRAISE:
1. Pat meat dry and season generously with salt and pepper
2. Heat oil in a heavy pot (Dutch oven) over high heat
3. Sear meat on all sides until deeply browned — do not skip this step
4. Remove meat, sauté aromatics (onion, carrot, celery, garlic)
5. Deglaze with wine or stock, scraping up browned bits
6. Return meat to pot — liquid should come halfway up the meat, not cover it
7. Cover tightly and cook at 300-325°F (150-160°C) for 2-4 hours
8. Meat is done when fork-tender and pulls apart easily

KEY PRINCIPLES:
- Low and slow — high heat makes tough meat tougher
- Liquid level: halfway up, not submerged (that is stewing)
- Tight lid traps steam and creates a self-basting environment
- Searing is not about sealing juices — it is about flavor through Maillard reaction

BEST CUTS FOR BRAISING:
- Beef: short ribs, brisket, chuck roast, oxtail
- Pork: shoulder, belly, hocks
- Lamb: shoulder, shank, neck
- Chicken: thighs and legs (not breast — too lean)

BRAISING LIQUID OPTIONS:
- Red wine + stock (classic beef braise)
- White wine + stock (chicken, pork, fish)
- Beer (short ribs, pork)
- Tomatoes + stock (Italian-style)
- Milk or cream (pork loin alla latte)
