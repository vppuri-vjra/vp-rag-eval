TOPIC: Marinating

Marinating is the process of soaking food in a seasoned liquid before cooking to add flavor and, in some cases, tenderize the texture.

WHAT A MARINADE DOES:
- Adds flavor to the surface and slightly below
- Acid-based marinades partially denature surface proteins (slight tenderizing)
- Oil carries fat-soluble flavors into the food
- Does NOT penetrate deeply — most flavor stays on the surface

THREE COMPONENTS OF A MARINADE:
1. Acid (tenderizes, adds brightness): citrus juice, vinegar, wine, yogurt, buttermilk
2. Fat (carries flavor, prevents sticking): oil
3. Flavoring (herbs, spices, aromatics): garlic, ginger, herbs, soy sauce, chili

MARINATING TIMES:
- Fish and seafood: 15-30 minutes only — acid "cooks" fish, making it mushy
- Chicken pieces: 2-8 hours
- Whole chicken: 8-24 hours
- Pork: 2-12 hours
- Beef steaks: 2-8 hours (longer can make texture mealy)
- Beef tough cuts: up to 24 hours
- Vegetables: 30 minutes to 2 hours

DOES MARINATING TENDERIZE?
Only surface-level. Acid breaks down surface proteins but does not penetrate more than a few millimeters. For deep tenderizing, brining or slow cooking is more effective.

ENZYME-BASED TENDERIZING:
- Pineapple contains bromelain — breaks down protein aggressively
- Papaya contains papain — similar effect
- Kiwi contains actinidin
- Use sparingly and for short time — can turn meat mushy

SAFETY RULES:
- Always marinate in the refrigerator — never at room temperature
- Never reuse marinade that has been in contact with raw meat as a sauce — food safety risk
- To use as a sauce: boil the marinade for at least 1 minute first
- Use glass, ceramic, or zip-lock bags — avoid reactive metals (aluminum) with acidic marinades
