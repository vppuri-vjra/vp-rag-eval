TOPIC: Sautéing

Sautéing is a dry-heat cooking method using a small amount of fat in a shallow pan over high heat. The word comes from the French "sauter" meaning "to jump."

PURPOSE:
- Cooks food quickly while developing flavor through browning
- Ideal for tender vegetables, thinly sliced meat, and seafood
- Creates fond (browned bits) on the pan bottom for building sauces

HOW TO SAUTÉ:
1. Heat the pan over medium-high to high heat before adding fat
2. Add fat (oil or butter) — it should shimmer immediately
3. Add food in a single layer — do not overcrowd
4. Allow food to sit undisturbed briefly to develop color
5. Toss or stir to cook evenly
6. Remove from heat as soon as food is cooked through

KEY DIFFERENCES FROM RELATED TECHNIQUES:
- Sautéing uses high heat and small fat — food cooks fast
- Pan-frying uses more fat and medium heat — food cooks slower
- Stir-frying uses very high heat and constant movement — no resting

FAT CHOICE:
- High smoke point oils (avocado, canola, vegetable) for high heat
- Butter adds flavor but burns above 300°F — use clarified butter for high heat
- Olive oil suitable for medium-high heat, not screaming hot

COMMON MISTAKES:
- Overcrowding the pan — food steams instead of browns
- Pan not hot enough before adding food — food sticks and steams
- Moving food too much — prevents browning (Maillard reaction)
- Using too much fat — becomes pan-frying not sautéing
