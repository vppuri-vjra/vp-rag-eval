TOPIC: Blanching

Blanching is a cooking technique where food is briefly submerged in boiling water, then immediately transferred to ice water to stop the cooking process. This is called "shocking."

PURPOSE:
- Preserves the bright color of vegetables (especially greens like broccoli and green beans)
- Partially cooks vegetables before freezing to deactivate enzymes that cause deterioration
- Loosens the skin of tomatoes, peaches, and almonds for easy peeling
- Removes bitterness from vegetables like kale or Brussels sprouts

HOW TO BLANCH:
1. Bring a large pot of salted water to a rolling boil
2. Prepare an ice bath (bowl of water with plenty of ice)
3. Drop vegetables into boiling water — do not overcrowd
4. Cook for 1-3 minutes depending on vegetable size and density
5. Remove immediately with a slotted spoon or strainer
6. Transfer directly to ice bath for the same amount of time as blanching
7. Drain and pat dry

TIMING GUIDE:
- Green beans: 2-3 minutes
- Broccoli florets: 2 minutes
- Spinach: 30 seconds
- Asparagus: 1-2 minutes
- Tomatoes (for peeling): 30-60 seconds until skin cracks

KEY TIPS:
- Use plenty of water so temperature does not drop when vegetables are added
- Salt the water generously — it raises the boiling point slightly and seasons the vegetable
- The ice bath must be ready before you start — timing is critical
- Blanched vegetables should be vibrant and slightly crisp, not fully cooked
