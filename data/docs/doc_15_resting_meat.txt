TOPIC: Resting Meat

Resting is the practice of letting cooked meat sit off heat for a period before cutting into it. It is one of the most important and most skipped steps in cooking meat.

WHY RESTING MATTERS:
During cooking, heat causes muscle fibers to contract and squeeze moisture toward the center of the meat. If you cut immediately, this moisture rushes out — you lose it to the cutting board. Resting allows fibers to relax and reabsorb moisture evenly throughout the meat.

WHAT HAPPENS DURING RESTING:
- Muscle fibers relax and expand
- Moisture redistributes from center to edges
- Internal temperature equalizes (carryover cooking continues)
- Result: juicier, more evenly cooked meat

CARRYOVER COOKING:
Meat continues cooking after being removed from heat. Internal temperature rises:
- Small cuts (steak, chops): 3-5°F rise
- Medium roasts: 5-10°F rise
- Large roasts (turkey, prime rib): 10-15°F rise
Remove meat from heat before reaching target temperature to account for this.

RESTING TIMES:
- Thin steaks (under 1 inch): 5 minutes
- Thick steaks (1+ inch): 8-10 minutes
- Pork chops: 5 minutes
- Chicken pieces: 5 minutes
- Whole chicken: 15-20 minutes
- Large roasts (beef, pork): 20-30 minutes
- Whole turkey: 30-45 minutes

HOW TO REST PROPERLY:
- Place on a cutting board or warm plate
- Tent loosely with foil — do not wrap tightly (traps steam, softens crust)
- Keep in a warm area — not back in the oven
- Do not skip resting to serve hot — a few minutes makes a significant difference

COMMON MYTH:
"Searing seals in juices." — FALSE. Searing creates flavor through the Maillard reaction, not a moisture barrier. Resting is what actually keeps meat juicy.
